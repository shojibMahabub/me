QA used to mean a ticket, a DBA, twenty minutes of waiting, and the risk that another tester was halfway through a case that would now be wiped.

We containerised the on-premises databases, seeded from a sanitised production snapshot, and handed every QA engineer their own spin-up-on-demand database.

The full story, including the numbers and what I would do differently, is written up here:

- [How Docker cut our QA time by 70%](/blog/docker-qa-70-percent/)

## The headline

- Average QA cycle went from about **35 minutes to about 10**.
- Environment-related bugs dropped to near zero.
- Regressions from "wrong data" in test envs became a memory.
