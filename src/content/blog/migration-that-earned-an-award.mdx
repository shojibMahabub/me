I’ve done plenty of migrations over the years, but this one was on a different level. It started with a single, deceptively simple sentence from a new client called Yes Bank: "If you can ship your code with Oracle, we’ll take this project." 

To a bystander, that might sound like a minor configuration tweak. For us at Veefin, it was a massive technical mountain. Our entire system was built on MySQL 7, and we were being asked to move it to Oracle 21c. This wasn't just a "find and replace" job; the depth of that request was huge.

You see, we had to process a massive amount of data for the bank at various cron timings throughout the day. Because of that complexity and the deep relationships in our data, we had avoided using the standard ORMs provided by Laravel. We relied on raw SQL and stored procedures to keep things performant. Everything we had written was perfectly friendly for MySQL, but Oracle has a completely different personality.

### The First Story: The "Translator" Library

The friction started early. We realized that simple things like the word `DATE`—which we used everywhere in MySQL—is a reserved keyword in Oracle. Then there were the differences in date formats, data types, and even how `GROUP BY` clauses behave. It was clear that we couldn't just flip a switch in the `.env` file and expect things to work.

Our team lead in Bangladesh was incredible during this period. At that time, he and our team were trusted over five or six other teams in India, and he pulled me in as part of the core unit to solve this.

> **Our First Major Move**
> We sat down and built a native PHP library specifically to modify our existing raw queries and make them Oracle-friendly on the fly. It was a massive team effort that allowed us to keep our core logic while satisfying the bank's requirements without rewriting thousands of lines of code.

### The Second Story: The Dusty PC in the Corner

While the library was a great start, the code was only half the battle. We hit a wall with reproducibility. With six of us working on these complex data flows, "it works on my machine" was becoming a constant refrain. We needed a central place where everyone could test against the same data in real-time.

I noticed an unused PC sitting in a corner of our Bangladesh office, collecting dust. I realized that if we could get that machine online, we could solve our collaboration bottleneck.

> **The Centralized Hub**
> With a few commands, I turned that forgotten hardware into a remote database server hosting both Oracle and MySQL. This meant our core team could work with the same data simultaneously. If someone found a bug or a data mismatch in an Oracle procedure, we could all see it and fix it in the library instantly.

### The Third Story: Ending the OCI8 Nightmare

But even with a central database, we had a bigger scaling problem. We had a team of over 20 developers and QA testers who all needed to connect to this new setup. If you’ve ever tried to get the `php-oci8` extension and the Oracle Instant Client to install properly across twenty different machines, you know it’s a special kind of hell. 

We were losing so much time. Developers were stuck in a loop of installation errors, environment variable mismatches, and broken library paths. We were supposed to be migrating a bank, but we were spending all our time troubleshooting local setups.

> **The Infrastructure Breakthrough**
> I spent time crafting a comprehensive Docker Compose file that handled the entire setup—the PHP environment, the OCI8 drivers, and all the necessary headers. Suddenly, what used to take days of troubleshooting for a new dev became a single command. It saved our 20+ developers and testers an incredible amount of time, finally letting the whole team focus on the actual migration.

In the end, we did it. We thoroughly tested the full system, and it worked flawlessly. The project was a success, and the effort we put in—both in the logic we built and the infrastructure we stood up—landed me significant internal recognition. 

Looking back, it wasn't just about moving data; it was about how we as a team simplified a complex problem so we could move faster than anyone expected. That’s how we earned the win.
