I've done plenty of migrations. This one was different because a bank was watching.

## The setup

Our supply chain finance platform ran on MySQL. Yes Bank India wanted to go live on it, but their policy required Oracle. We had one choice: move everything over — schemas, stored procedures, application code, background jobs, the ledger — while development on other banks kept going.

## Where we started

Weeks one and two were the humbling part. MySQL and Oracle disagree about small, important things: auto-increment keys vs sequences, JSON handling, timestamp precision, reserved words, case sensitivity. We spent days cataloguing every assumption our codebase made and wasn't aware of.

## Dual-writes and diffs

The play that saved us was building a dual-write shadow. For three weeks before the cut-over, every write hit both databases. Every night, a job walked 40+ critical tables, compared row counts and column-level checksums, and screamed at us in Slack if anything drifted.

It drifted twice. Once from JSON serialization (MySQL kept insertion order, Oracle did not — a problem when a downstream parser cared). Once from a DST boundary in a timestamp column. Both would have been nightmares in production.

## The cut-over

A 6-hour maintenance window. Freeze writes, run a final diff, flip the config, warm up the workers, unfreeze. The rollback script was one command. We wrote it early and never used it.

## What I actually learned

- A big migration is 90% discipline, 10% SQL.
- The diff harness is the product. The migration is a side-effect.
- Dual-write for longer than you want to. You'll find real bugs only by watching the two systems disagree for a while.
- Write the rollback script first. It focuses the mind.
